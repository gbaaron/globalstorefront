The Pitch — Step by Step

You are NOT closing a sale in person. You are opening a door. Your only goal is to get them curious enough to take a 15-minute call with Aaron. That's the whole job.

1

Start with a question, not a pitch

Don't open with "I sell websites." Open by being curious about their business. People talk to people who are interested in them.

2

Find the pain — then stop talking

Ask about their current site and then listen. "What's your biggest frustration with it?" is worth more than any pitch. The moment they name a problem, you have your angle.

3

Plant the seed — one or two sentences max

Tie what you do directly to the problem they just named. Don't explain everything. Just enough to make them ask "really, how does that work?"

4

Show the demo — on your phone, right now

Pull up the demo portal. A real working site beats any description. Let them tap through it. This is your strongest tool — use it every time.

5

Ask what they'd want

"If we built something like this for you, what features would matter most?" Gets them picturing it as their own — not just watching a demo.

6

Ask for the intro call — and be specific

"Can I set up a quick 15-minute call with my team? They'll show you exactly what it would look like for your business — no commitment, just a look." Get their number or email. That's the close.

Common Questions — and How to Answer Them

Tap any question to see how to handle it. You don't need to have every answer — "that's a great one for the call with the team" is a completely valid response.

How much does it cost?
Question

Most common question. Don't guess. Don't give a range. Redirect to the call where Aaron can give a real number based on their specific needs.

Say this:
"It really depends on what features you need — a simple site versus one with ordering, a loyalty program, and a full admin dashboard are totally different projects. The team will give you an exact quote after a 15-minute call. What I can tell you is it's a one-time build, not a monthly fee that goes up every year."
If they push hard: "Most custom projects run in the low-to-mid thousands. But let Aaron give you the real number — he's done enough of these that it's a 10-minute conversation."
How long does it take to build?
Question
Say this:
"Usually 2–4 weeks. And here's the thing — you get access to a private preview link as it's being built, so you can see it come together in real time and give feedback. Nothing goes live until you're happy with it."
The preview portal is a big differentiator. Most agencies disappear for a month and show up with something you didn't expect. We don't.
Can I update it myself?
Question
Say this:
"Yes — you get a custom admin dashboard built for your specific business. You can update your menu, manage orders, see your stats — all from your phone. No coding, no developer needed for day-to-day stuff. If you can use Instagram, you can use the dashboard."
What if I need changes later?
Question
Say this:
"You own the code outright — so any developer can make changes, including our team. Day-to-day updates like menu items or business hours you handle yourself through the dashboard. Bigger changes, we're available."
This is the opposite of Wix — where you're stuck on their platform forever and can never take the code anywhere.
I already have a website. Why do I need a new one?
Question

Don't trash their current site. Reframe from "you have a site" to "does it actually do anything for you?"

Say this:
"Totally — most of our clients did too. The real question is: is it actually bringing in business, or is it more of an online business card that just exists? A custom site with ordering or booking built in is a tool that works for you 24 hours a day."
Do I need to know anything technical?
Question
Say this:
"Not at all. You tell us how your business works — what your customers do, what would make your day easier — and the team builds around that. You don't need to understand how it works. You just need to use it."
What if something breaks or goes down?
Question
Say this:
"Support is available from the team. And because you own the code and it's hosted independently — you're not at the mercy of a platform going down or deciding to change their pricing. You're not going to wake up and find out Squarespace tripled their fees."
What's the difference between this and hiring someone off Fiverr?
Question

Don't be dismissive of Fiverr — it's a real option. Your point is accountability and reliability.

Say this:
"You can — and a lot of business owners have tried that. The issue usually comes up 6 months later when something breaks, the person's gone, and nobody knows how to fix it. We build it, we stand behind it, and you have someone to actually call."

Objections & How to Handle Them

An objection is not a no. It's usually a question in disguise. Most objections mean "I'm interested but I have a concern." Dig for the real one.

"It's too expensive."
Objection

Don't argue. Don't agree either. Find out what they're currently paying and reframe the math.

Say this:
"I get that. Can I ask what you're currently paying? Wix, Squarespace, any ordering apps, booking tools — it adds up more than people realize. And those are monthly forever. This is a one-time cost."
For restaurants on DoorDash: "If you're doing $8,000/month in delivery orders at 25% commission — that's $2,000/month going to them. A custom ordering system could pay for itself in the first month."
"I'm happy with what I have."
Objection
Say this:
"That's fair. Just out of curiosity — is it actively bringing in new customers on its own, or is it more of an online business card that just exists? Because there's a real difference between a site that exists and one that actually does something."
If they're genuinely happy with a working site, they may not be the right prospect right now. Plant the seed and move on. They might circle back later.
"I don't have time for this right now."
Objection
Say this:
"Totally understand. It's a 15-minute call and the team handles everything after that — you don't build anything yourself. When would be a better time for me to come back? Next week?"
Always try to book a specific follow-up time rather than leaving it open-ended. "I'll come back sometime" almost never happens.
"I need to think about it."
Objection

Usually means there's a specific unspoken concern. Try to find it.

Say this:
"Of course. What would make the decision easier — is it the price, or is there something specific you'd want to see first?"
If it's price: "The call is literally just to get you an exact quote. 15 minutes, no commitment. At least you'd have a real number to think about."
"I need to talk to my partner / spouse / manager."
Objection
Say this:
"Absolutely. Would it help to set up the call with both of you? That way all the questions get answered at once and neither of you has to explain it to the other. I can ask the team to schedule it whenever works."
"My nephew / friend builds websites — I'll just have them do it."
Objection

Be respectful. Don't trash their nephew. Focus on accountability and the complexity of the features.

Say this:
"That's great — if they can build what you need, that works. The sites we build aren't just design — they have real backend systems like live ordering, inventory, dashboards. It's a more complex build than most people expect. If your person can handle that, perfect. But if you ever want a second opinion on what it would take, the call is free."
